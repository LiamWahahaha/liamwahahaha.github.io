2D transformations

  • Translation

    2D translations can be written as $\bold{x’} = \bold{x} +\bold{t}$

  • Rotation + translation

    This transformation is also known as 2D rigid body motion or the 2D Euclidean transformation (since Euclidean distances are preserved). It can be written as $\bold{x’} = \bold{Rx} + \bold{t}$

  • Scaled rotation

    Also known as the similarity transform, this transformation can be expressed as $\bold{x’}=s\bold{Rx}+\bold{t}$ where s is an arbitrary scale factor.

    The similarity transform preserves angles between lines.

  • Affine

    The affine transformation is written as $\bold{x’} = \bold{A \bar{x}}$, where $\bold{A}$ is an arbitrary $2 \times 3$ matrix.

    Parallel lines remain parallel under affine transformations.

    An affine transformation does not necessarily preserve angles between lines or distances between points, though it does preserve ratios of distances between points lying on a straight line.[2]

  • Projective

    This transformation, also known as a perspective transform or homography, operates on homogeneous coordinates, $\bold{\tilde{x’}} = \bold{\tilde{H}\tilde{x}}$, where $\bold{\tilde{H}}$ is an $3 \times 3$ matrix.

    Perspective transformations preserve straight lines (i.e., they remain straight after the transformation).

  • Hierarchy of 2D transformations

  • Stretch/squash

    This transformation changes the aspect ratio of an image,

    $$x’ = s_xx+t_x$$

    $$y’ = s_yy+t_y$$

    , and is retricted form of an affine transformation.

  • Planar surface flow

    • This eight-parrameter transformation arises when a planar surface undergoes a small 3D motion.

    • It can be thought of as a small motion approximation to a full homography.

  • Bilinear interpolant

    This eight-parameter transform can be used to interpolate the deformation due to the motion of the four corner points of a square.
